Q1: If revenue grows 10% but COGS grows 15%, what happens to gross margin?
Correct! When COGS grows faster than revenue, the gap (gross profit) shrinks as a percentage of revenue — so gross margin decreases. Example: Revenue $739.6M (+10%), COGS $328.1M (+15%), Gross Profit = $411.5M = 55.6% margin vs. original 57.6%.
Q2: Nova's net margin is 27.3%. Is that high or low for a semiconductor equipment company?
Very high. Industry average is ~10–15%. Nova is exceptionally profitable thanks to its software-intensive metrology tools, high switching costs, and strong pricing power.

Q: Nova's Operating CF is $235.3M but Net Income is $183.8M. Why is CF higher?
Correct! Net income includes non-cash expenses (like depreciation ~$17.4M). When calculating cash flow, these are added back because no cash actually left. Also, changes in working capital affect cash flow. OCF = Net Income + D&A ± Working Capital changes.

Q: The balance sheet always balances. What is the fundamental accounting equation?
Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Always. This is the bedrock of double-entry accounting. Every transaction affects at least two accounts and keeps this equation in balance. Nova: $1,400M = $700M + $700M ✓
Step 4: How the 3 Reports Connect
The three statements are not independent — they flow into each other. Click each box to highlight the connections.

P&L → Cash Flow

Net Income is the starting point of the Cash Flow statement. D&A and working capital changes are then added/subtracted to arrive at Operating CF.

P&L → Balance Sheet

Net Income flows into Retained Earnings (part of Equity). Profitable companies grow their equity over time.

Cash Flow → Balance Sheet

CAPEX (from investing activities) becomes a long-term asset on the Balance Sheet. Cash used for CAPEX reduces the cash balance.

Q: Which margin ratio do investors focus on most when comparing companies?
EBITDA Margin is the most widely used for cross-company comparisons because it strips out non-cash charges (D&A) and the effects of financing structure (interest, taxes). This makes companies with different capital structures or tax situations comparable on an apples-to-apples basis.

What If: If DSO jumps from 65 to 90 days, what happens to Nova's cash position?
Nova would need ~$46M more cash tied up in receivables: [(90−65)/365] × $672.4M = $46.1M. That's $46M less cash available for share buybacks, R&D investment, or acquisitions — a real cost even though it doesn't show up on the P&L.

Open Discussion

Nova trades at ~20.7x EBITDA. A direct competitor trades at 8x EBITDA. What are 3 possible explanations?
1. Nova has higher growth expectations — analysts forecast faster revenue growth.
2. Nova has a stronger competitive moat — its process control technology is mission-critical with high switching costs.
3. Nova operates in a more profitable niche (metrology/process control) with software-heavy revenue.
4. Scarcity premium — few comparable pure-play companies exist, so investors pay up for exposure.
